Michael Burry Warns Bitcoin Crash Could Trigger 'Cascading Liquidations' Across Finance

The investor who predicted the 2008 financial crisis says Bitcoin's deep integration into leveraged structures creates systemic risk.
Sam Reynolds Feb 4, 2026, 3:42 PM

Michael Burry, the hedge fund manager famous for predicting the 2008 housing collapse and immortalized in "The Big Short," has warned that Bitcoin's ongoing decline could trigger cascading liquidations across leveraged financial portfolios.

In a Substack post published Monday, Burry argued that Bitcoin's recent sharp decline is "something beyond a normal bear market," describing it as "a uniquely dangerous setup that could trigger cascading financial turmoil."

"Bitcoin is now deeply embedded into leveraged structures — from corporate balance sheets to ETF derivatives to margin trading platforms. Further price drops will force more selling, creating a vicious cycle."

Burry pointed to data showing that 44% of Bitcoin's circulating supply is now underwater, meaning holders are sitting on losses. He warned this creates pressure for capitulation selling.

The investor also noted correlations between crypto and precious metals markets: "It looks like up to $1 billion in precious metals were liquidated at month's end as a result of falling crypto prices. The crypto-precious metals correlation has created sickening scenarios."

Key Data Points

• Bitcoin down 40% from October 2025 all-time high of $126,080
• 44% of BTC supply now underwater (Glassnode)
• $410B+ wiped from total crypto market cap in 2026
• Fear & Greed Index at 14 (Extreme Fear)

While some dismissed Burry's warnings as alarmist, others noted his track record of identifying systemic risks before they materialize. Burry famously bet against subprime mortgages in 2007-2008, a trade that netted his fund over $700 million.

"The difference between this cycle and previous crypto winters is institutional involvement," said Elena Rodriguez, chief risk officer at Galaxy Digital. "When MicroStrategy, Tesla, and spot ETFs hold hundreds of billions in Bitcoin, a cascade is no longer just a crypto problem — it's a Wall Street problem."
